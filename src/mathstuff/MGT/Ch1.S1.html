1.1.  Preliminary Exercises

Ex 1.1.1.

Read the proof of [ART91, App. A] (every vectorspace has a basis.)

Ex 1.1.2.

Show that the dimension of any vector space is well defined. (Hint: every element in one basis is generated by finitely many elements of the other.)

Ex 1.1.3.

Generalise the definition of a basis to a module, and define free modules to be modules that have a basis. Then show that for each cardinal α, there is a free module on α generators (i.e, there is a free module with a basis having cardinality α) (Hint: Put an R−module structure on {f:α→R|f⁢ finitely supported}).

Ex 1.1.4.

Show that the dimension of any free R−module F is well defined. i.e, any two bases for a free module have the same cardinality. (Hint: Let I be a maximal ideal of R. Investigate the R/I−module F/I⁢F.).11 1 Another important question is the following: Let M be an R−module. When is M naturally an R/I−module? That is, if the R−action on M is given by the map ⋅R:R×M→M, when is the action ⋅R/I:RI×M→M given by (c+I,x)↦c⋅Rx well defined? give your answer in terms of Ann(M):={a∈R:(∀x∈M:a⋅Rx=0)} by showing that I⊆Ann⁡(M)

Ex 1.1.5.

If R is an integral domain, show that any free submodule M of a free module F has dim(M)≤dim(F)

Ex 1.1.6.

If R is a PID, show that any submodule of a free R−module is also free. (Hint: Zorn’s lemma. See [LAN12, App. 2] for full proof.)

Ex 1.1.7.

Let f:M→N be a split surjection (i.e there exists a map α:N→M such that f∘α=i⁢dN). Then show that M=α⁢(N)⊕ker⁡f. Figure out and prove a dual statement for an injective map f:M→N.

Ex 1.1.9.

An R−module P is said to be projective if for all tuples (M,N,f,g) where M,N are R−modules, f:M→N a map making the bottom row exact and g:P→N a map, there is a map h:P→M such that the following diagram commutes.22 2 Reversing the arrows gives the dual notion of injective modules.:

Show that if P is a projective module, then

  1. a)

    every surjective map to P splits.

  2. b)

    Show that if P⊕P′ is projective, then P,P′ are projective. Show that free modules are projective. Conclude that P is projective iff it is the direct summand of a free module (i.e there exists Q such that P⊕Q is free.)

  3. c)

    Show that (R×{0})⊕({0}×S)≅R×S as R×S−modules.

  4. d)

    Use the above exercises to find a projective module that is not free.

Note: From the previous results, we can conclude that all projective modules are free if R is a PID.

Ex 1.1.10.

An R−module N is said to be noetherian if one of the following equivalent conditions holds:

  1. 1.

    Every ascending chain of submodules N1⊆N2⊆⋯ stabilises. i.e, (∃m)⁢((∀k≥m)⁢(Nk=Nm)).

  2. 2.

    Every nonempty collection of submodules of N (ordered by inclusion) has a maximal element.

  3. 3.

    Every submodule of N is finitely generated.

Show that the above conditions are equivalent.

Ex 1.1.11.

Show that M is a noetherian R−module iff there exists a submodule N such that N and M/N are finitely generated (Hint: Look at the second isomorphism theorem.)

A ring R is said to be noetherian if R is a noetherian R−module. What does this condition translate to in terms of R and its ideals?

Ex 1.1.12.

Let R be a noetherian ring. Show that M is a noetherian R−module iff M is finitely generated. (Hint: Show it for free modules. To do it for free modules, use the previous exercise.)

Ex 1.1.13.

If A,B,C are rings such that A⊆B⊆C with A,C noetherian, then is B noetherian? is every UFD noetherian?

Ex 1.1.14.

Let I be an R−ideal. Define the colon ideal (I:a)={x∈R:ax∈I}.Now assume that I+a⁢R is generated by X∪{a} with X⊆I and (I:a) is generated by Y. Then show that X∪{a⁢y:y∈Y} generates I.

Ex 1.1.15.

Prove the following lemma:

Lemma 1.1.1 (Cohen).

R is noetherian iff every prime ideal of R is finitely generated.

(Hint: Suppose not for contradiction. Use Zorn’s lemma and the previous exercise.)

Ex 1.1.16.

Define for I⊆R the radical I={x∈R:(∃n∈ℕ)⁢(xn∈I)}. Show that this is an ideal. Now assume that R is noetherian. Show that there exists n with (I)n⊆I.

Ex 1.1.17.

Let R be noetherian. Show that every R−ideal contains a product of finitely many prime ideals. (Hint: Assume not for contradiction. Then a certain collection becomes nonempty.)

Ex 1.1.18.

Let R be a noetherian ring, and let M be an R−module. If I is a proper ideal, show that there exists R/I↪M iff ∃0≠x∈M with Ann⁡(x)=I. Show that there exists 𝔭 prime with R/𝔭↪M.
