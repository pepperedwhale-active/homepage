4.  Homotopy Invariance

As we have seen before, two spaces being homotopy equivalent implies that their fundamental groups are isomorphic. We see the same in the case of singular homology groups. Infact we have a slightly better result:

Theorem 4.1.

If f0,f1:X→Yf_{0},f_{1}:X\rightarrow Y are homotopic maps, then for each p≥0p\geq 0, the induce homomorphisms (f0)∗,(f1)∗(f_{0})_{\ast},(f_{1})_{\ast} are equal.

Proof.

I will not show the full proof here, one can read [LEE10] for that. But the proof is nice enough that I think providing atleast a sketch must be done. First we reduce the problem to the case Y=X×IY=X\times I, f0⁢(x):=i0⁢(x)=x↦(x,0)f_{0}(x):=i_{0}(x)=x\mapsto(x,0) and f1⁢(x)=i1⁢(x):=x↦(x,1)f_{1}(x)=i_{1}(x):=x\mapsto(x,1), by simply using the functoriality of taking the fundamental group. Then we note that in order to show this, it suffices to find homomorphisms h:Cp⁢(X)→Cp+1⁢(X×I)h:C_{p}(X)\rightarrow C_{p+1}(X\times I) such that

h∘∂+∂∘h=(i1)♯−(i0)♯.h\circ\partial+\partial\circ h=(i_{1})_{\sharp}-(i_{0})_{\sharp}.

This is simply because for any boundary ∂c\partial c, we have h∘∂(∂c)+∂∘h⁢(∂c)=∂∘h⁢(∂c)∈ker⁡(∂)h\circ\partial(\partial c)+\partial\circ h(\partial c)=\partial\circ h(% \partial c)\in\ker(\partial).

the key fact used to construct hh is that a p−p-prism can be constructed as a sum of some p−p-simplies. So the term ∂∘h\partial\circ h constructs the bonudary of the prism, and the term h∘∂h\circ\partial cancels out the sides, giving us the difference of the top and the bottom. Lee has a nice graphic of this in his book [LEE10]. ∎
